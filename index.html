What is K-Means Clustering?

K-Means is an unsupervised machine learning algorithm that partitions data into K distinct clusters based on similarity.

Key Steps:

  1. Initialization: Randomly select K centroids.
  2. Assignment: Assign each data point to the nearest centroid.
  3. Update: Calculate new centroids as the mean of assigned points.
  4. Repeat: Continue until centroids no longer change.

Advantages:

  • Efficient and fast for large datasets.
  • Simple and intuitive.
  • Works well with spherical clusters of similar size.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires choosing K clusters beforehand.
  • Sensitive to initial centroid selection.
  • Struggles with outliers and non-spherical clusters.

Real-World Applications

Here are some real-world examples of K-Means clustering applications:

  • Customer Segmentation: Companies use K-Means to group customers based on purchasing behavior, demographics, or interests, allowing for targeted marketing and personalized services.
  • Image Compression: K-Means is used to reduce the number of colors in an image by grouping similar colors together, which helps in compressing image files while maintaining quality.
  • Anomaly Detection: K-Means helps identify outliers by detecting data points that do not fit well into any cluster, making it useful in fraud detection and network security.
  • Document Clustering: K-Means can group similar documents or text based on content, which is useful in organizing large datasets, such as news articles or research papers.
  • Market Basket Analysis: In retail, K-Means can group items often purchased together, helping businesses with inventory management and promotional strategies.
  • Healthcare: K-Means is used in medical research to cluster patients with similar health conditions or symptoms, aiding in diagnosis and treatment plans.
  • Geographical Clustering: It helps group locations based on factors like population, climate, or crime rates for urban planning and resource allocation.
